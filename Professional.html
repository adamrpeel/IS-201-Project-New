What Was Spinosaurus?

Spinosaurus aegyptiacus was an enormous semi-aquatic theropod dinosaur that lived approximately 95–100 million years ago during the Cretaceous period in what is now North Africa. Estimated at 46–59 feet long, it surpasses even Tyrannosaurus rex and Giganotosaurus in length, making it the largest known carnivorous dinosaur in Earth's history.

First discovered in Egypt in 1912 by German paleontologist Ernst Stromer, the original fossils were tragically destroyed during World War II. It wasn't until the 2000s—and especially the 2020 discovery of a near-complete tail in Morocco—that scientists began to truly understand this extraordinary creature.

Quick Facts & Classification

  1. Taxonomy
    • Kingdom: Animalia
    • Order: Saurischia
    • Family: Spinosauridae
    • Genus: Spinosaurus
    • Species: S. aegyptiacus
  2. Physical Stats
    • Length: ~46–59 ft (14–18 m)
    • Mass: ~7–20+ metric tons (estimates vary)
    • Neural spines (sail): up to 5.4 ft (1.65 m) tall
    • Skull: elongated, crocodile-like, ~5.7 ft long
  3. Time & Place
    • Period: Late Cretaceous (~95–100 Ma)
    • Location: North Africa (Morocco, Egypt, Algeria)
    • Environment: River deltas and tidal flats
  4. Diet
    • Primarily piscivorous (fish-eating)
    • Likely opportunistic — carcasses, smaller animals
    • Isotope studies confirm aquatic feeding behavior

Anatomy & Adaptations

Reconstruction of Spinosaurus in its river environment (Art: Davide Bonadonna / National Geographic

The Iconic Sail

The most recognizable feature of Spinosaurus is the row of enormous neural spines extending from its vertebrae. These spines may have supported a sail-like structure for thermoregulation and display, or a thick, muscular hump like a modern bison. Current evidence leans toward the sail hypothesis.

Built for Water

Spinosaurus had dense bones (reducing buoyancy), short hind limbs, large paddle-like feet, and a long sinuous tail with tall spines — strongly suggesting it was a capable swimmer. Its nostrils were positioned high on the skull so it could breathe while partially submerged, much like a crocodile.

The Skull

The elongated, narrow skull is packed with conical teeth — perfect for catching slippery fish. Pressure-sensitive pores on the snout, similar to those in modern crocodilians, likely helped it detect prey movement in murky water.

World of the Kem Kem

Spinosaurus lived in the Kem Kem ecosystem of North Africa — a vast river system teeming with life. This was one of the most predator-dense environments in Earth's history, sometimes called the "River of Giants."

🐊 Carcharodontosaurus

A massive land-based theropod that shared the ecosystem — roughly T. rex-sized, it likely occupied a different ecological niche on land while Spinosaurus dominated the waterways.

🐟 Onchopristis

A giant saw-shark up to 26 feet long. Spinosaurus teeth are often found alongside Onchopristis scales, strongly suggesting this was a primary food source.

🐢 Giant Turtles

Several species of enormous freshwater turtles lived in Kem Kem rivers, providing another potential food source for the river-dominating Spinosaurus.
